- **Mobile: the hero image is cut off at the bottom of the viewport.** User has to scroll to see the full product shot. The CTA is visible which is good, but the product itself -- the thing you're selling -- is partially hidden.

- **Trust bar text at 13px is too small** and uses uppercase, making it hard to read quickly.

## Overall Page Assessment

### Visual Rhythm
**Score: 4/10.** The alternating background strategy is not working. The stated pattern in the CSS comments (cream, dark marquee, white, cream, white, indigo, white, cream, indigo, white, gray, white, indigo) is not what actually renders. In practice, large swaths of the page are the same light lavender (`#E8E0F0`), creating a monotonous, washed-out middle section. The empathy anchor, product intro, three pillars, trust chorus, buy box, origin story, FAQ, and reviews sections all either use lavender or cream backgrounds that are barely distinguishable from each other. Only the comparison chart, guarantee, and newsletter sections break the pattern with dark indigo. **The page needs more aggressive background alternation: white, then cream, then white, with dark indigo used for 2-3 key moments.**

### Hierarchy of Importance
**Score: 5/10.** The hero is identifiable as the most important element, and the comparison chart commands attention. But the buy box -- the single most important conversion element -- is visually subdued on a lavender background with a "Sold out" state. The hierarchy should be: Hero > Buy Box > Comparison > Social Proof > Everything Else. Currently, the buy box is buried in position 9 of 15 sections.

### Story Flow
**Score: 7/10.** The narrative arc is well-conceived: hook (hero) > empathy (statistics) > solution (product intro) > simplicity (how it works) > superiority (comparison) > social proof (trust chorus) > purchase (buy box) > risk reversal (guarantee) > brand (origin) > objection handling (FAQ) > more proof (reviews) > community (newsletter). This is textbook DTC funnel copywriting. The problem is not the story -- it is the visual execution of that story.

### Single Biggest Design Weakness
**The overwhelming lavender monotony and lack of visual contrast in the middle 60% of the page.** Sections 3 through 14 (empathy anchor through FAQ) blur together into one long, light purple mass. There is no visual rhythm, no "breathing" moments, no white space breaks. This makes the page feel endless and undifferentiated, causing scroll fatigue. Every benchmark brand (AG1, Ritual, Seed, Olipop) uses stark white sections alternating with accent-colored or dark sections to create clear visual chapters.

### Single Strongest Design Element
**The comparison chart section.** The dark indigo background, the horizontal bar visualization, the teal hero bar, and the sharp competitor subtitles combine to create a section that is immediately understandable, visually striking, and persuasive. This section alone could convert skeptics. It should be the template for the overall page's visual confidence.

## Competitive Benchmark Comparison

### Where DairyAssure Falls Short vs. AG1 / Ritual / Seed / Olipop

1. **Photography quality.** Benchmark brands use exclusively real, professionally shot photography. DairyAssure relies on AI-generated imagery that undermines credibility. This is the widest gap.
2. **Visual contrast and rhythm.** Benchmark brands use 3-4 distinct background colors with clear section boundaries. DairyAssure's lavender-dominant palette creates a monotone experience.
3. **Sticky navigation + persistent CTA.** All benchmark brands have a fixed header with a "Shop" or "Subscribe" button visible at all times. DairyAssure's header scrolls away.
4. **Price storytelling.** AG1 leads with cost-per-day ($2.79). Ritual shows monthly price with savings. DairyAssure shows only "$17.50" with no framing.
5. **Motion and interactivity.** Benchmark brands use subtle scroll animations, video content, and interactive elements. DairyAssure is entirely static.

### Where DairyAssure Excels

1. **Copywriting.** The headlines, subheads, and microcopy are sharp, benefit-driven, and emotionally resonant. "Enjoy Dairy Without the Drama," "Trust your gut," and "One Ingredient. Zero Compromise" are professional-grade lines.
2. **Information architecture.** The section order tells a complete story from problem to solution to proof to purchase. The funnel logic is sound.
3. **Comparison chart.** This section is best-in-class execution. It clearly communicates superiority without being aggressive.
4. **Brand voice consistency.** Every section speaks in the same warm, confident, slightly playful voice. There are no tonal shifts.

## Top 5 Priorities for Loop 2

### 1. Fix the "Sold Out" Buy Box (CRO -- Critical)
**Impact: Extreme.** Every CTA on the page leads to a dead end. Either mark the product as in-stock (if this is a staging issue), or replace the buy box with a "Notify Me When Available" email capture with strong copy. This alone is blocking all conversions.

### 2. Break the Lavender Monotony (Visual Design -- High)
**Impact: High.** Introduce stark white (`#FFFFFF`) backgrounds for the empathy anchor, three pillars, trust chorus, and reviews sections. Use cream (`#FAF7F2`) for the product intro and FAQ. Reserve lavender (`#E8E0F0`) for at most one section (origin story). This single change will transform the page from a purple blur into a structured, readable experience.

### 3. Increase Hero Heading Size + Add Sticky Header (Typography + CRO -- High)
**Impact: High.** Bump the desktop hero heading from 40px to 52-56px. Add a sticky header with a visible "Shop" or "Try Now" CTA button that appears on scroll. These two changes will improve first-impression impact and provide a persistent conversion path.

### 4. Replace AI Photography with Real or High-Quality Stock (Brand Trust -- High)
**Impact: High.** The AI-generated lifestyle photos in the Trust Chorus and Origin Story sections are visibly artificial. Replace with real customer photos (even UGC), professional product photography, or carefully selected stock that doesn't trigger the "AI uncanny valley." Until real photos are available, consider removing the lifestyle images and letting the copy + product shots carry the sections.

### 5. Add Price Framing to Buy Box (CRO -- Medium)
**Impact: Medium.** Show "$0.35 per stick" or "Less than a cup of coffee per meal" alongside the $17.50 price. Add a subscribe-and-save option (even if it's a future feature, mock the UI). Add quantity-based savings tiers ("2-pack: Save 10%"). Frame the value, don't just state the price.
